1. Data Cleaning and Preprocessing

The raw dataset contained redundant imperial-unit columns, inconsistent country names in multiple languages, and extreme outlier values across key weather metrics.

Steps Taken

  • Removed 8 redundant imperial-unit columns (Fahrenheit, mph, inches)
  • Parsed last_updated datetime and extracted year, month, day, hour, season
  • Capped outliers using IQR winsorizing on 9 key numeric columns
  • Mapped 211 countries to continents including multilingual names (French, German, Arabic, Chinese, Cyrillic)
  • Verified 0 missing values and 0 duplicates in final dataset
Final cleaned dataset: 144,822 rows x 33 columns with 0 missing values

2. Exploratory Data Analysis

Temperature

Global average temperature is approximately 21°C with a right-skewed distribution. Africa and South America are the warmest continents. Russia, Canada, and Nordic nations are consistently the coldest.

Precipitation

Precipitation is highly right-skewed — the majority of observations record 0mm. Oceania and South America show the highest average precipitation by continent.

Key Correlations

  • Temperature and feels_like_celsius: near-perfect correlation (r=0.99)
  • Temperature and UV index: strong positive correlation
  • Temperature and humidity: moderate negative correlation
  • PM2.5 and PM10: strong positive correlation (same pollution source)
  • Wind speed and PM2.5: negative correlation (wind disperses pollutants)

3. Anomaly Detection

Two unsupervised anomaly detection algorithms were applied to identify extreme weather events across the dataset.

Isolation Forest (Full Dataset)

Applied to all 144,822 rows using 8 weather features. Detected 4,345 anomalies representing 3% of the dataset.

Local Outlier Factor (Sample)

Applied to a 20,000-row sample for computational efficiency. Confirmed the 3% anomaly rate independently.

Anomaly Profile vs Normal Observations

Anomalies are characterized by significantly higher wind speeds (+49%) and doubled PM2.5 pollution levels, consistent with storm events and industrial pollution episodes.

4. Forecasting Models

Three models were trained on daily global average temperature with a 60-day test set. Lag features (1, 7, 14, 30 days) and rolling statistics were engineered for the ML models.

Model MAE RMSE R2 MAPE
ARIMA(5,1,2) 2.1427 2.4541 -3.1300 10.12%
Linear Regression 0.2967 0.3579 0.9122 1.45%
XGBoost 0.2870 0.3639 0.9092 1.39%
ARIMA's negative R2 reveals that classical statistical models struggle with globally-aggregated temperature data. The signal lacks the autocorrelation structure ARIMA requires, while ML models with lag features capture the pattern effectively.

5. Ensemble Models

Three ensemble strategies were tested: simple average, inverse-MAE weighted average, and Ridge regression stacking.

Model MAE RMSE R2 MAPE
ARIMA 2.1427 2.4541 -3.1300 10.12%
Linear Regression 0.2967 0.3579 0.9122 1.45%
XGBoost 0.2870 0.3639 0.9092 1.39%
Simple Average Ensemble 0.7711 0.9093 0.4330 3.64%
Weighted Average Ensemble 0.3165 0.3901 0.8957 1.52%
The weighted ensemble assigned approximately 98% of its weight to Linear Regression and XGBoost combined, effectively neutralizing ARIMA's poor performance. A 2-model ensemble of LR and XGBoost would likely outperform all approaches tested.

6. Climate Analysis

  • A slight warming trend was detected over the 2-year observation period
  • Africa and South America are the warmest continents on average
  • Europe shows the strongest seasonal temperature variation
  • Southern hemisphere seasons are inverted relative to the Northern hemisphere
  • Tropical zones average 5 to 10 degrees warmer than temperate zones
  • Polar regions show the highest temperature variability month to month

7. Environmental Impact

  • Asia has significantly higher PM2.5 and PM10 levels than any other continent
  • Wind speed has a strong negative correlation with PM2.5 — wind disperses pollutants
  • Humidity has a positive correlation with PM2.5 — moisture traps particles near ground level
  • Visibility decreases sharply as PM2.5 increases
  • The majority of global observations fall in the Good or Moderate AQI category
  • Carbon Monoxide shows seasonal peaks consistent with heating and industrial activity

8. Feature Importance

Four methods were used to rank feature importance for temperature prediction: Random Forest, XGBoost, Permutation Importance, and SHAP values. All four methods produced consistent rankings.

Rank Feature Reason
1 feels_like_celsius Direct temperature proxy — accounts for wind chill and humidity

3 wind_kph Affects perceived and actual temperature through convection

9. Spatial Analysis

  • Interactive choropleth maps confirm Middle East and Sub-Saharan Africa as hottest regions
  • South and East Asia dominate global PM2.5 pollution maps
  • Folium heatmap shows a clear temperature gradient from equator to poles
  • K-Means clustering (k=5) identified 5 distinct global weather profiles
  • Strong negative correlation confirmed between latitude and average temperature
  • Weather stations in tropical latitudes show the least seasonal variation

Key Findings

  • XGBoost with lag features significantly outperforms classical ARIMA for global temperature forecasting
  • Anomalous weather events show doubled PM2.5 levels and 49% higher wind speeds vs normal days
  • Asia has substantially worse air quality than all other continents across every metric
  • Temperature is primarily driven by seasonal timing and humidity rather than pressure or wind alone
  • Ensemble models are only effective when base models have comparable performance levels
  • Global weather stations cluster into 5 distinct climate profiles that align with known climate zones
